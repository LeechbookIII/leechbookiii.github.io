116v

Old English
Modern English
xxvi. Wiþ miclanlice genim nioþowearde elenan and þung and ompran þa þe swimman wile ealra emfela and gecnua wel and wyl on buteran do wel sealtes on and smire mid.
xxvi. Against big-body27 take the lower part of elecampane and monk's hood and yellow pond lily, all equally, and pound well and boil in butter. Add in a good [portion of] salt28 and smear with [it].
Wyrc bið wiþ þa miclanlice elene. ælfþone. marubie. curmealle. ellentanas. and actanas. wyl swiðe on wætre and beþe on swiðe hatum þæt lic.
Make a bath against the big-body. Boil elecampane, bittersweet nightshade, white horehound, knapweed, elder twigs and oak twigs vigorously in water and bathe the body in a very hot [bath].
Wyrc drenc wið þam miclanlice hindhioloþan. curmeallan. bogen. nefte. agrimonia. betonica. finul. dile. do on god ealo sele drincan on dæge .iii. scencas fulle.
Make a drink against that big-body. Put hindheal, knapweed, rosemary, cat's mint, agrimony, betony, fennel, dill in good ale. Give three cups full to drink during the day.
Wyrc briw wiþ þon ilcan genim niotowearde elenan and eoforþrotan. redic and þa readan netlan nioþowearde scearfa smæle gecnua wel wyl siþþan on buteran do clæne ifig taran þær on gif þu hæbbe and hwon berenes melwes do on blede mid þam wyrtum and hrer mid sticcan oþþæt hit col sie sele etan on neaht nestig .iii. snæda sele þone briw and þone drenc ær þam bæþe þy læs hit inslea æfter þam baþe.
Make a pottage against the same. Take the lower part of elecampane and stemless carline thistle, radish and red nettles, the lower part. Cut into small shreds. Pound well. Boil afterwards in butter. Put clean ivy tar thereon if you have it and a little barley meal. Put in a bowl with the herbs and stir with a spoon until it is cool. Give three morsels to eat after night's fast. Give the pottage and the drink before the bath lest [the big-body] strike after the bath.
xxvii. Wit singalum dust untrumra manna. Nim wermod and hindhioloþan and gyþrifan wylle on ealaþ geswete hwon sele him drincan hit hælþ þone þurst
xxvii. Against the continual dust from sick people, take wormwood and hindheal and corncockle. Boil in ale. Sweeten a little. Give [it] to him to drink. It will heal the thirst
27. There is still some debate over what "miclan lice" refers to. It is often translated as elephantitis, but Cockayne relates this specific remedy also to leprosy. Olds' footnote to the remedy provide greater detail to the connection between elephantitis, leprosy and "miclan lice." ↩
28. Cockayne: "add a good spice of salt..."
Olds: "Add sufficient salt..." ↩
